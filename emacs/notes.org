** keymaps.el
*** philosophy (top-level)
Main principles:
1. top-level binds > vim binds
   a. emacs is more than a text editor. the most important keys are the top-level ones i use in /all/ modes, e.g. switching buffer/window, GNU Readline -style keys for word/line kill/nav.
   b. note: some core vim keys (navigation) are promoted to top-level, e.g. C-u/C-d, C-e/C-y, C-w, g-.
   c. note: some top-level keys replace vim ones, e.g. s-<left/right> for fwd/back instead of C-i/C-o.
      - when this happens, C-i and C-o can become top-level keys. This is great! ("o" and "i" are prime real estate!).
2. reduce cognitive load w/ consistent interfaces
   a. the goal is a consistent interface no matter the mode. E.g. C-c C-p enables writable mode for /both/ grep and dired.
   b. spacemacs claims to be an exemplar here, and to have invested tons of hours. i should check it out. finding the patterns would be pretty cool.
   c. modern apps are good inspiration, too. E.g. s-, for "Settings" has been great.
3. lean into s- and C- when comfortable
   a. s- and C- are each adjacent to my L and R thumb's default resting positions of shift and space, repsectively.
   b. 2-key chords with 1 key per hand are very comfortable /and fast/. (this is a valid exception to anti-RSI conventional wisdom that leader sequences are king).
   c. counterexample: i'd like to give spacemacs-style leader sequences a try eventually. we'll see.
4. use <E> when necessary
   a. type 1: hyper-bespoke menu-style modes (e.g. magit). 
   b. type 2: repl modes (agent-shell, vterm). they don't play well in insert mode.
   c. note: good top-level binds help here a lot. e.g. C-i to switch window.
5. apapt modes to <N> or <M> otherwise
   a. e.g. dired in <N>: it's basically <E> but with j, k, g, /.
      - (not the best example tbh, as there's not many keys)
      - it could also be <M> since it's readonly (could switch to <N> for wdired mode)
   b. e.g. help and info in <M>: keep j, k, g, /. readonly mode, so most other keys can be free.
